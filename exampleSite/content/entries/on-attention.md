There's a particular feeling, sometime around the third hour of poking at a
bug, when the bug stops being a thing in the code and becomes a thing in
your head. The variables, the call stack, the half-remembered StackOverflow
thread — they all stop being external and start being part of the way you're
thinking, the way an experienced driver stops noticing the steering wheel.

This is, I think, what attention costs: it asks you to let the world push
itself into the shape of your mind, and to let your mind take the shape of
the world. It's why deep work feels expensive. It's also why it feels good
when it goes well.

<!--more-->

We talk about "paying attention" as if it were a transaction with a clear
price. But what we're really paying with is the option to be doing
something else.

## The cheap kind

There is a cheap kind of attention, and the internet has gotten very, very
good at extracting it from you. It is the attention you give to a notification,
to a brightly coloured thumbnail, to the small social hit of a reply. It
costs you almost nothing in the moment, but you pay for it later: it
fragments the longer kind of attention, the kind that lets a problem unfold
inside you over hours.

I have nothing original to say about this. It has been said better
elsewhere. I only want to note that this is the problem of our era as
clearly as smog was the problem of the 19th century — a free-to-emit
externality that ruins a shared commons.

## The expensive kind

The expensive kind is what you give to a single piece of code, a single
piece of prose, a single conversation, for long enough that it changes
shape. This kind of attention is what produces understanding. It is also
what produces craft: the only way I have ever learned anything is by
staring at it for too long.

I don't know how to defend it except crudely. Close the tabs. Put the phone
in a drawer. Pick one thing.
